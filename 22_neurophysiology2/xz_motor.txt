Hierarchy
    Higher: strategy (what movement do I want?)
    Middle: tactics (what muscle sequence do I need to use?)
    Low: execution
Sensorimotor: requires sensor input to properly adjust, at the three levels
Neuron types
    Upper motor: all components in CNS. The ones directly contacting lower motor neurons
        Damage here keeps reflexes intact
    Lower motor neuron: execution neurons. Soma in CNS, axons synapse outside it
        Damage here loses the muscle, as it degenerates when not used
Cortical afferents not just from M1, also from the other motor cortices
Specialized pyramidal neurons for each movement feature: direction, speed, force
    Final direction is proposed to be the vector sum of all the activated neurons
M1: the one that contains upper motor neurons
    Subject to plasticity
Premotor: more associative. Direct stimulation does not cause movement
    Info from other cortices, outputs to the primary motor cortex
Mirror neurons: function of imitation. Fires when executing a movement, or watching another execute the same movement
    Discovered in premotor cortex, but in many other places, too
Supplementary motor area. Coordination of movement sequences
    Active when thinking about movement
    Responds to internal cues (vs. premotor that responds to external cues)
    Fire more instants before the actual execution of the movement
    Seem bilateral - both sides activate upon stimulus
Descending tracts
    Lateral corticospinal (the one that crosses) => distal parts, precise movement
    Ventral corticospinal (the one that does not) => proximal parts, gross movement
    Corticobulbar: same but for CNs
    Rubrospinal: less function in humans, may compensate corticospinal tract damage
    Pons reticulospinal: support antigravitational machinery
    Medullary reticulospinal: inhibition of the antigravitational machinery
ALS: degenerates upper motor neurons

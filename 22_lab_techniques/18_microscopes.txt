Airy disk: blur in microscope due to refraction
    Sets a limit to the level of detail (resolution) that a microscope can get
    The smaller wavelength (colder colors), the smaller the airy disk
        But higher wavelength penetrate better
    Electron microscopy removes this problem
    If your volume is big (e.g. entire organs), stereomicroscopy or light sheet
    Tissue (3-10 um): widefield, confocal
        Confocal achieves more detail
    Cells and large organelles (0.2-10um)
        If you just need to see big things (e.g. nuclei): widefield
        If you need to see small organelles with precision: confocal
    Colocalization: are 2 proteins living at the same time in the same place?
        Use confocal
    Confocal uses a potent laser and kills cells
    Deconvolution: removes out-of-focus light (post-processing)
    Nano-structures (smaller than diffraction limit): super-resolution microscope, electron microscopy
Cryo-EM: the most potent microscopy technique ATM
Contrast: ability to distinguish what we want to see against the background
    Transparent live samples
        phase contrast: for the contour
        differential interference contrast: for volume
    Transparent fixed samples: TEM
    Specific protein or structure (in its original place): fluorescence (with optical microscopy)
    Opaque objects: SEM
Microscope config
    Upright: watching the sample from above. Vs. inverted
Working distance: distance from lens to sample
Lens
    Chromatic aberration: distortion because != wavelengths refract differently
        Achro: corrects this
    Spherical aberration: distortion because a planar target can't focus on a planar surface
        Fluar: corrects this
    Magnification depends on the lens but also on the eyepiece lens
    Tube length: from lens to eyepiece lens, typically infinite for variable size today
    Numerical aperture: measures the ability to capture diffracted light. Greater magnifications => greater NA
        NA = sin(alpha) * diffraction_index
        Dry objectives: limit 1 (because n=1 for air)
        Objectives using oil: limit 1.5
    Immersium medium: what media can be used with? (lmm = multi-immersion, W = water, oil = oil, gly = glycerine)
        Important because if we introduce media with n != n_glass, rays scatter
        Also coded as a color
    Coverslip thickness: standard is 0.17mm, but all of them are manufactured
        Collar may help, but objectives have an optimal width
    DIC: suitable for polarized light
Mounting
    Always count, because you need to have always the same number of cells per experiment for reproducibility
    Adherent: place the coverslip in the bottom of the plate, then let the cells grow
        Cultivate in a plate, measure how many
        Calculate how many you'd need to have a 70% confluence in the well
        Pipette 1/2 of this because cells double each day (approx.)
        Leave the well for a day
    Non-adherent cells: need coating to stick to the surfaces
        Glycocalyx is anionic, but glass is, too => coating needs to be cationic
        Submerging the coverslip in HCl overnight helps to 'clean' it
        Or cover the coverslip with ECM compounds, like collagen (but it's expensive)
Fixing
    Cross-linking: creates links between proteins and within protein residues
        Formaldehyde: it's a gas - sold as formalin: liquid + saturated with formaldehyde gas + methanol (but methanol is a fixative, too)
        Paraformaldehyde: polymerized, solid form of formaldehyde. Powder, should be added a base and heated
            But the vapors are dangerous
            PFA is methanol free, better than formalin
        Gluteraldehyde: for EM
        May fix too much and block epitopes required for immunology-based fluorescence
    Coagulating fixatives: makes protein precipitate
        Preserves immuno detection, but doesn't preserve morphology
        Methanol does this
    Don't fix too much, otherwise you can't cut slices
Permeabilization: required if you need immunological techniques
    Because antibodies are too big to cross the membrane => Make holes
    With cross-linking fixatives only
    Use detergents at low concentration
        Triton X-100: also perforates nuclear envelope, if you want your antibody to enter everywhere
        NP-40 or digitonin: if you don't want the antibody to enter the nucleus
Staining
    Dyes: binding to acid or basic components
    Fluorescence: binding to a particular molecule
    Organelle staining dyes: can cross membranes without breaking them, and bind depending on the organelle
    Plasma membrane: using fluorescently linked lectins => bind to the glycocalyx
    MitoTracker: for mitochondria. Reliant on membrane potential. First dye, then fix (because fixation removes membrane potential)
    BODIPY ceramide: fluorescent lipid that accumulates in Golgi. First dye, then fix (because fixation stops Golgi transport)
    DAPI and Hoetsch: bind to DNA => stain nucleus
    Antibodies are cleaner in general
    Block: prevent non-specific interactions. Ideally, 10% FBS
Mounting media: prevent air because of the != refraction index
    E.g. mobiol

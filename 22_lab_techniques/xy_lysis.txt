Homogenization: make the sample homogeneous in composition so we can pick reproducible samples from it
Note: things with cell wall are more difficult to break
Harsh
    Lysate: fluid produced by a lysis technique
    Mild homogenization: break cell clumps (homogenization at cell level)
        Vs. harsh, ending up in lysis
    Abrasives: grind with beads to break the tissue
        Poor reproducibility, although can be made with industrial equipment
    High-pressure: use a press. Make the material pass through a small hole using pressure
    Blades: for tough tissue
    Sonication: ultrasounds that generate cavitation
        Higher intensity and lower frequency => more strength. You want enough strength to break cells, but not enough to burn proteins
        You need to control time because it generates heat (and in localized spots)
    Osmosis: place in hypotonic medium (e.g. distilled water) => cell explodes
        Used with red blood cells
Mild: preserve organelles and nucleus
    Trituration: pass it through an embolo
        Medical needle gauge system: bigger number, thinner needle
        No bubbling, it kills cells
    Enzymatic tissue digestion: remove ECM
        Commercial cocktails of enzymes (e.g. collagenases, trypsin...)
        Cut, wash with PBS, digest at 37ºC, then strain to clean up debris
Lysis
    Mechanical: tends to preserve contents better, in theory
    Enzymatic: specific enzyme that digests cell membranes
        Lysozyme: breaks peptidoglycan => breaks cell walls
        Used with hypotonic solutions => break the cell membrane underneath
        Other enzymes target specific components in cell walls of specific species
        Proteinase K: breaks nuclear envelope and digests DNAses and RNAses
        Expensive - no one does it
    Chemical: use pH or detergents
        Cheap and popular
        But the chemicals may interfere with downstream procedures - might need removal
        Don't work well with thing with cell wall
        Detergent-based
            Surfactant = detergent
            Structure is similar to physiological lipids => can destroy membrane lipids
            Tend to form micelles (hydrophobic tail inside, hydrophilic heads outside, in a circular fashion)
            Detergent molecules get in place of phospholipids and make pores
            Stabilizes hydrophobic proteins usually embedded in the plasma membrane
                Solubilization. Instead of aggregating when the membrane dissolves, they are still in solution keeping their properties
                One micelle by protein
            Ionic detergents: "harsh" because they tend to denaturalize proteins (hydrophilic head is charged)
                Sodium Dodecyl Sulfate (SDS): negatively charged
                The negative charge disturbs ionic and hydrogen bonds, but not covalent bonds
                Net charge of the micelle is negative
                CTAB: positively charged
                    Used to "kill" prokaryotes because their membrane has negative charges on the outside
                pH affects how they work => don't use if you need extreme pH values
                Can't be used if you need to operate with high concentration of other salts => because salt charges interact with the ones in the detergent
            Non-ionic detergents: hydrophilic head is not charged
                More popular because they don't affect the structure of proteins
                Exact chemical name not important
                Triton X-100: breaks the nuclear envelope. Also used for prokaryotes
                NP-40: used to lyse mammalian cells. It doesn't break nuclear envelope
                Glucosidic units: sugar as the hydrophilic tail
                    OG: used to crystalize proteins for X-ray diffraction
                    DDM: for membrane-embedded proteins
                    Digitonin: breaks membranes containing cholesterol => Good to keep organelles intact
                        Cholesterol is abundant in plasma membrane (rigid), but not so much in internal organelles (not so rigid, e.g. ER)
            Zwitterionic detergents: both positive and negative charge in the molecule
                In the middle of ionic and non-ionic detergents
            Capacity to solubilize hydrophobic things proportional to the capacity to denaturalize proteins
        pH-based: not used in the lab, but used in commercial kits
            Used together with a detergent
            Alkaline: NaOH + SDS. Isolating plasmids
            Acidic: inorganic acid. Isolating RNA
    Keeping proteins intact: more problematic than nucleic acids
        You need a pH buffer and an ion donor (salt)
        Because there are many proteases in cells that might get activated during lysis
        Protease inhibitors: usually commercial cocktails. Many proteases and an inhibitor for each one
        We expose proteins to oxygen => Oxidized (e.g. proteins start making disulfide bonds and aggregate)
        Alkylating agents: bind to certain amino acid side chains to prevent aggregation
        Blocking phosphatases is needed, even if you're not interested in phosphorylation cascades
            Because phosphorylation state may change how the protein is targeted by other proteins
        Temperature control is needed => work in ice, otherwise the enzymes work faster than inhibitors
            Except for SDS, because it precipitates
        Depending on proteins, add other stabilizers: e.g. glycerol, sucrose

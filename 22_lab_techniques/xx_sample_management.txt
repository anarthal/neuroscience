Collection: follow std procedure
    Samples must be labeled
    Human data anonymized
        Total: all PII removed or encrypted
        Partial: some data (e.g. age, gender) conserved
        Pseudoanonymization: data mapped to code, code mapping stored encrypted
    The most used in pseudo anonymization
    In the container, date and all info that might be useful for handling
    For plasmids, plasmid name and gene inserted, but not bacterial strain if we just work with ecoli
    Accompanying form: a form in the lab stating the details about a stored sample
Storage: cryopreservation (liquid nitrogen, slow down the metabolism)
    Cells, tissues, organs: -196ºC
    Hooks, with boxes attached to them, in a tank full of liquid N
    Cryo-damaging: cell exploding because water freezes and increases volume
    DMSO: prevents crystal formation but is toxic. ~50% cells die. Freezing point decreases
        Has a polar area
        Shields protein structure
        Toxic because it gets inside the membrane and makes holes, converts alpha helices into beta sheets, and generates ROS in mitochondria
        10% for cell lines, 5% for delicate cells like primary cells
        Toxicity happens more at higher T (> º0C)
    Osmotic shock: water flows out of the cell because of the ice crystals formed outside
        Happens if you freeze the cell too slowly
    FBS or FCS (fetal bovine/calf serum): buffer to dampen ROS (albumin 'sacrifices itself' to remove ROS)
    Freeze slowly, thaw quickly and remove the DMSO
    Defrost by rubbing with hands or in hot water bath
        Then dilute in medium to reduce DMSO concentration, then centrifuge to eliminate DMSO
    Mr Frosty (cryocooler): reduce T to -20ºC at a rate of 1ºC/min (has isopropanol in the bottom to achieve this)
        Then to -80ºC freezer, dry ice, then -196ºC for long-term storage
    Glycerol: similar to DMSO, but less toxic. Used for bacteria and cells used for clinical purposes
        DMSO is smaller, so it permeates better => You need to wait less before freezing
        More osmotic imbalances. But bacteria is more protected to this because of the cell wall
        Bacteria has glycerol transporter that expels glycerol => more protected
        Mg2+ protects bacterial membranes because LPS are negatively charged, and Mg2+ shields them
        ~25%-35%
    Bacteria stored at -80ºC
    Spermatozoan also have glycerol transporter => glycerol used to store them
    Fixed samples: paraffin embedded at 4ºC (for samples not to be used alive)
    Proteins: mix with protease inhibitors and stored at -20ºC (short-term use) or -80ºC (long-term use), or lyophilized (TBC)
        Plastic vials (short term) or glass (long term), because proteins stick to plastic
        Freeze and thaw cycles degrade molecules => divide the sample into individual aliquotes
    Lipids: dissolve in non-water solvent (e.g. methanol), store at -20ºC in crystal vial, use gas to avoid oxygen degradation, no light
    Sugars: tend to be contaminated with microbes. 4ºC (for simple sugars) or -20ºC (for complex sugars)
    RNA usually in 0.5ml because they fit in the PCR machine
Lab registers
    Each box in the freezers has an associated sheet stating what's in the tube

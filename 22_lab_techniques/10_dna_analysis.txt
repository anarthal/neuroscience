Sequencing
    Sanger
        ddNTP: modified nucleotide linked to a fluorophore that inhibits further polymerization
        Can only sequence small segments, up to 1kb
        Reading longer sequences: 'walking primer'. Design a primer with the nucleotides in the ending sequence
PCR
    Tm (melting): temperature at which 50% of primers are annealed
    Ta (annealing): temperature at which primers are annealed
        should be Tm - 5ºC
        Too high: primers don't prime
        Too low: primers prime too much
    Tm = f(G/C, num_nucleotides)
    num_nucleotides should be [17, 45]
        Too many: Tm gets closed to 95ºC
    G/C should be [0.4, 0.6]
    Tm should be [60, 75] ºC
    Temperature of extension and denaturation are constant
    Time of extension varies: f(polymerase, length of the fragment)
        E.g. Taq does 1000bp/min, but makes more mistakes; Pfu 400bp/min but makes less mistakes
        Speed is constant
    1st denaturation is longer because you need to denature the entire template
    Usually [30, 35] cycles
    Final extension cycle at the end to finish any unfinished molecules
R-PCR
    cDNA: coding DNA. No introns present
    Purify RNA, then use reverse transcriptase, then PCR (needs the usual sequence-specific primers)
    Oligo-dTs: RNA primers that locate the poly-A tail so only mRNA is reverse-transcripted
        They sometimes don't amplify the full mRNA. If the downstream PCR primers are located within the 5' end of the gene, they may fail
    Random primers: 6 nucleotide random primers that amplify randomly
        But they are random - sometimes it doesn't work
    You can mix them
qPCR: quantitative
    Relative quantification: how much DNA vs. a control
    Ct value: the cycle in which the fluorescence value exceeds a certain threshold
Molecular cloning
    Insert: the piece of DNA you want to insert into the plasmid
    Promoter direction in the plasmid is relevant: you don't want to clone the gene in reverse order
    Cutting the plasmid: select 2 restriction enzymes in the plasmid and digest the plasmid
        Each enzyme has a != buffer type, so each cute in a separate iteration
        Then you place it in gel electrophoresis to extract the small DNA fragment that you've cut
    To cut the gene of interest: similar to a PCR, but your primers have the restriction enzyme sequence at the sides
    Use a DNA ligase (from a virus) to seal the gene to the plasmid
    Then either sequence or digestion analysis

ELECTRONEGATIVITY: ability of an element to attract electrons in the context of a chemical bond
BIOMOLECULE: molecule composed mainly by C and H
In a peptide bond, the oxygen pulls the electrons => C has more density of negative charges on the other side => peptide bond is double bond like
Conditionally non-essential: can be produced by our body, but not at the same rate as the others
You always write N=>C
Basic group: amino, acid: carboxyl
In normal conditions, amino acids are ionized, but net charge = 0
    Zwitterion: when a molecule has both positive and negative charges (ionizations). Does not imply that the net charge is 0
Non-polar side chains: they are not ionized and net charge is zero
    Proline: has a cycle between residue - amine => can't rotate as freely
Uncharged polar: net charge is zero, but have electrostatic effects
    Cysteine: has a thiol group, very reactive under oxidative stress => forms a disulfide bond if another cysteine is close
Thiol group: HS
Outside the cell we have oxidative conditions
    Extracellular proteins usually have cysteines because it helps stabilize
Charged: side chains are positively charged (basic), or negatively charged (acid)
pK: constant of equilibrium. pH at which 50% of an entity is ionized
Histidine: the only one that has pK close to physiological => part ionized, part not ionized
Amino-acids are "buffers" (they take or give protons, depending on pH)
Isoelectric point (pI): pH at which amino-acid net charge is zero
    For alanine: mean(pK1, pK2)
Alpha helix can only form if the residues allow it. E.g. too many glycines (small)/triptophans (big) don't fit
    Proline's N is too 'busy' to form a H bond
Alpha helix has stricter constraints than beta sheet

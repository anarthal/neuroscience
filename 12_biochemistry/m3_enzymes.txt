BPG: metabolic product of glycolysis
Relaxed hemoglobin has more affinity for O2
Bohr effect: low pH, high CO2 => less hemoglobin affinity for O2 => O2 released in tissue
Haldane effect: complementary to Bohr
BPG reduces hemoglobin affinity for oxygen
    High metabolic activity (high O2 needs) => increased BPG concentration => hemoglobin releases O2
2,3-DPG: decreases hemoglobin affinity for oxygen
    Doesn't have an effect in fetal Hb
Polycythemia: abnormal production of red-blood cells => increases blood viscosity and may cause thrombos
Michaelis-Menten equation for reaction velocity
    1st order reaction: velocity proportional to the concentration of substrate
    [S]: substrate concentration
    Low [S]: 1st order reaction
    As [S] increases, the curve flattens (saturation)
K_d: dissociation constant = k_-1 / k1
K_a: association constant = k1 / k_-1
The higher K_d, the less affinity the enzyme has for the substrate
In an enzymatic reaction we have 2 factors
    Affinity: how well does the enzyme bind to the substrate
    Catalysis: how well does the enzyme create the product
K_m = (k_-1 + k_2) / k_1 (combines both affinity and catalysis)
    Experimentally: the substrate concentration at which reaction velocity reaches 1/2 the maximum velocity
Inhibition
    Competitive: competes with the substrate for the binding site
        At low [S], it doesn't change much. Effects appear at higher [S]
        K_m changes
        Maximum velocity doesn't change - asymptotic velocity remains the same, but requires higher [S]
    Non-competitive: inhibits the reaction but doesn't compete with the substrate. E.g. changes the conformation of the enzyme
        Maximum velocity is affected because the enzyme is altered
        K_m not altered (?)
    Uncompetitive: compound that binds the enzyme-substrate complex and doesn't allow the reaction to move forward
        

Regulation time scales
    Fast: changes to existing molecules
        Allosteric changes (seconds)
        phosphorylation (minutes)
    Slow: changes in gene expression and hormones
    Irreversible: e.g. proteolytic cleavage
        E.g. trypsinogen => trypsin (produced by pancreas, activated in small intestine, for protein digestion)
Every enzyme has an optimal
    pH
    T (usually the higher, the better, to a point where the enzyme denaturalizes)
